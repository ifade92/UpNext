# UpNext Brand Voice Profile

## The Thesis (read this first — it anchors everything else)

**UpNext is a digital sign-in sheet for barbershops.**

That's it. That's the whole product.

The clipboard worked fine when shops had 2 chairs. It stopped working around 5. Names got missed. Walk-ins crowded the barber by the door asking "how long?" The list became a guess.

We didn't reinvent the sign-in sheet. We didn't try to. We just upgraded it for shops that grew past what a clipboard could handle:

- **The barbers can read the list from their phone** instead of squinting across the shop
- **Walk-ins can see where they are in line** instead of asking every five minutes
- **The wait time is real** instead of a shrug
- **Names don't get missed** because customers sign themselves in

That's the upgrade. That's UpNext. Familiar tool, working better.

This thesis shapes every word of UpNext content. We never sound like we're reinventing the shop. We sound like we made the most basic tool in the shop finally catch up to the shop.

## Who UpNext Talks To

**Shop owners running 5-10 chairs.** Usually the founder. Often a barber themselves who built the shop up. Still cuts hair some days, runs the business the rest. Knows every barber by name, knows the regulars, knows which days are chaos.

They're not corporate. They didn't go to business school. They figured this out the hard way — through long hours, mistakes, and watching their margins. They're proud of what they've built and protective of it.

They have specific pains: the sign-in sheet nobody scratches names off of, walk-ins crowding the chair closest to the door asking "how long?" while that barber is mid-cut, customers leaving silently because the wait was a mystery, the daily argument over who was actually next, appointment customers and walk-ins blending into one confusing line.

They don't need to be educated about *why* their problem is a problem. They live it. They need a way out that respects their intelligence.

They've also seen a hundred SaaS companies promise to "transform" their shop. They're tired of it. The voice has to earn their trust by sounding like one of them.

## The Product (anchor for all content)

**UpNext is a digital sign-in sheet that walk-ins fill out themselves and barbers read from their phone.**

Three ways to sign in, no app to download:

1. **Scan a QR code** on the poster or on the Live Queue display
2. **Check in remotely** through the live link, with a 30-minute arrival timer
3. **Use the iPad kiosk** if your shop has one set up

After they sign in, customers see the queue on their phone. They can stay, run an errand, grab coffee, come back. They make the call — because they finally have the information.

UpNext also handles appointment check-ins. Customers book through whatever site each barber uses (Square, Booksy, etc.) — UpNext doesn't replace that. But when the appointment customer arrives, they scan the same QR and pick their barber. Now the barber knows their 2pm is in the lobby, and the rest of the shop knows who's a walk-in versus who's waiting for someone specific.

For the shop: an accurate list, a real wait time, and the barber closest to the door gets to actually cut hair instead of fielding questions all day.

For the customer: information. They've never had it before. Now they do.

## The Founder Story (the pivot)

This is real, and it's a powerful piece of UpNext's voice. Use it.

UpNext didn't start as a digital sign-in sheet. It started as something more complicated — a separate queue for each barber, complex routing logic, a whole system. Carlos was overbuilding it.

Then he pulled back. Looked at his own shop. The barbers were using a clipboard. The clipboard worked, mostly. It just couldn't keep up with a 15-barber Saturday.

So he scrapped the complicated version. Rebuilt UpNext as the simplest possible thing: a sign-in sheet, digitized, with a few useful upgrades on top. Replaced the clipboard. Didn't reinvent it.

This story matters because it's *the same lesson every shop owner has lived*. Everyone has over-complicated something. Everyone has had to simplify back. When UpNext tells this story, it tells shop owners "we get it" without saying those words.

## What UpNext Sounds Like

**1. A peer, not a vendor.**

UpNext was built *inside* a barbershop, by an actual owner. The voice reflects that. We talk to shop owners the way one shop owner talks to another over coffee — straight, no jargon, no "synergy," no LinkedIn-speak.

> ✅ "You know the barber whose chair is closest to the door? The one who ends up answering 'how long?' between cuts? Yeah. We built UpNext for that barber."
>
> ❌ "Leverage our intelligent queue management platform to optimize customer throughput."

**2. Confident but not cocky.**

We use UpNext ourselves. We don't oversell — we show. We don't promise the moon — we promise specific, real outcomes.

> ✅ "Customer scans the QR. Their name's on the list. They see the wait time. They decide if they're staying or coming back. That's the whole walk-in flow."
>
> ❌ "Revolutionary AI-powered queue intelligence that transforms your shop."

**3. Specific over generic.**

Numbers, names, real moments. Not "increase efficiency." Not "delight customers." Real shop language, real shop scenarios.

> ✅ "Saturday at 11:23am. Four names on the clipboard. Seven people standing around the chair by the door."
>
> ❌ "Improve your busiest hours."

**4. Evolution, not disruption.**

UpNext respects what came before. The clipboard worked — for a long time, in a lot of shops. We're not here to bury it. We're here to upgrade it for the shops that outgrew it.

> ✅ "The clipboard had a good run. It just couldn't keep up with a 7-chair Saturday."
>
> ❌ "Stop using outdated, broken systems!"
>
> ✅ "Same sign-in sheet. Better tools."
>
> ❌ "Revolutionize your shop's operations!"

**5. Information as the whole value.**

UpNext doesn't make customers wait less. It makes them wait *informed*. We don't promise speed. We promise visibility. That's the whole game.

> ✅ "Nobody minds waiting. They mind not knowing."
>
> ❌ "Cut wait times by 30%!"

**6. A little dry, a little funny, never trying too hard.**

We don't do exclamation points stacked three high. We don't do emojis-as-personality-replacement. When something's funny, we let the situation be funny without underlining it.

> ✅ "The clipboard had a good run. 1987 to 2026. Survived by the QR code."
>
> ❌ "OMG say goodbye to clipboards forever 😱🔥💯!!!"

**7. Respects the chair.**

UpNext is not here to disrupt the craft. We handle the front-of-shop noise so the chair work stays sacred.

> ✅ "Your barbers should be cutting, not refereeing the line."
>
> ❌ "Streamline your operational workflow."

## What UpNext Never Sounds Like

- **Reinventing the wheel.** The sign-in sheet is the wheel. We just upgraded it. Don't write copy that suggests UpNext is something *new and different* — write copy that suggests UpNext is something *familiar that finally works at scale*.
- **Tech bro / Silicon Valley.** No "10x," "disrupt," "revolutionary," "game-changer," "AI-powered." If we use technical words, we earn them.
- **Corporate / B2B SaaS.** No "leverage," "synergy," "optimize," "stakeholders," "best-in-class," "industry-leading," "platform."
- **Hype-bro / guru.** No "ATTENTION SHOP OWNERS 🚨" or "this changed my LIFE" or "you won't BELIEVE." We don't shout.
- **Patronizing.** No "Did you know?" intros, no explaining the obvious. Shop owners know their problems. We don't lecture.
- **Generic small-business marketing.** No stock photos of diverse smiling teams shaking hands. No "empowering local businesses." We're specific to barbershops.
- **Apologetic.** We don't soften. We don't hedge. UpNext works. Speak like it does.
- **Promising things UpNext doesn't do.** No "we'll text your customers." No "automated reminders." No "AI predictions." No "we replace your booking system." Don't write copy that implies we do.
- **Talking about a "front desk" or "reception."** Most barbershops don't have one. There's just the barber whose chair is near the door.
- **Saying walk-ins pick a barber.** They don't. Walk-ins go on the list and get the next open chair. Only appointments are tied to a specific barber.

## Vocabulary

**The thesis-level word:** **sign-in sheet** (or *the list*). This is what UpNext *is*. Use it generously, especially in headlines and openings.

**Product names UpNext uses (use these exactly):**

- **Live Queue** — the in-shop TV display showing the queue + QR code (split-screen)
- **The QR code** — the entry point. Just call it "the QR code." Not "QR-based check-in system."
- **The live link** — the URL that shows the queue from a phone. "Send your customers the live link."
- **Remote check-in** — the flow where someone signs in from home with a 30-minute timer
- **The kiosk** — the optional iPad setup. Mention it; don't lead with it.
- **The barber app** — the iPhone app the barbers use to manage the list

**Words UpNext uses freely:**

sign-in sheet, the list, walk-in, appointment, queue, the wait, sign in, check in, scan, the chair, the lobby, the regulars, your barbers, your shop, owner, build, run, ship, fix, busy day, slow day, Saturday, 11am, real-time, accurate, see, know, the barber by the door, the chair closest to the door, clipboard

**Words UpNext avoids:**

clients (we say *customers* or *regulars*), users, platform, solution, leverage, optimize, streamline, seamless, revolutionary, robust, enterprise-grade, intelligent, smart (as in "smart queue"), AI, journey, experience-as-a-noun, notify, text, SMS, alert, reminder, front desk, reception, receptionist, booking (when implying we book — we don't), transform, disrupt, reimagine, reinvent

**Be careful with:**

- *Wait time* — we show it, we don't reduce it. UpNext makes the wait *visible*, not shorter.
- *Notify / alert / remind / text* — we don't do these yet. Skip them entirely until we ship them.
- *Pick your barber* — only true for appointments. Walk-ins don't pick.
- *Booking* — UpNext does NOT book appointments. It checks them in. Each barber has their own booking site.
- *Kiosk* — fine to mention, but the QR code is the headline.
- *Reinvent / transform* — never. UpNext is not reinventing anything. It's upgrading.

## How to Talk About Walk-ins vs. Appointments

UpNext serves both, but they're different stories. Marketing should lead with walk-ins (the bigger, louder problem and the more distinctive UpNext angle). Appointments come up as a complement, not a co-headline.

**Default posture:** Most posts are walk-in stories. Every 4th or 5th post can mention the appointment side, especially when the angle is "UpNext lets your barbers see who's waiting for *them* vs. who's a walk-in."

**Never frame it as:** "UpNext for walk-ins AND booking!" Because we don't book.

**Do frame it as:** "UpNext handles walk-ins and appointment check-ins on one list. Booking stays where you've got it — Square, Booksy, wherever."

## Tone Across Post Types

**Product / Feature posts:** Specific, demonstrative. Show the thing, name the moment it solves.

> "Walk-in scans the QR on the wall. Types their name. They're on the list. They see the wait — 22 minutes — on their phone. They decide if they're staying or coming back. That's the whole flow. The sign-in sheet, but useful."

**Customer wins / social proof:** Quote the shop owner. Don't paraphrase. Real numbers, real names (with permission).

> "Mike runs a 7-chair shop in Houston. He stopped using a clipboard four weeks ago. His words: 'Walk-ins stopped crowding the chair by the door asking how long. They just look at their phone. My guys are cutting again.'"

**Behind-the-scenes / founder:** Carlos's hand, but in UpNext's voice. Real, specific, no false humility.

> "I started building UpNext as something complicated. A queue per barber, routing logic, the works. Then I looked at my shop. We were using a clipboard. The clipboard worked — it just couldn't keep up. So I scrapped the complicated version and built a digital sign-in sheet instead. That's UpNext. That's all it is."

**Educational / industry:** Smart but not preachy. Observation, not lecture.

> "Most shops don't lose walk-ins because the wait is long. They lose them because the wait is invisible. People don't mind waiting 45 minutes. They mind walking in, asking 'how long?' and getting a shrug from the barber mid-cut."
